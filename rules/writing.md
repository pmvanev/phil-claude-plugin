# Writing Guide

## Strunk & White's *The Elements of Style* — Summary

*The Elements of Style* by William Strunk Jr. and E.B. White is the definitive guide to clear, concise English writing. First published in 1919, it remains the gold standard for writers seeking precision and elegance.

---

### Core Philosophy

> **"Vigorous writing is concise. A sentence should contain no unnecessary words, a paragraph no unnecessary sentences, for the same reason that a drawing should have no unnecessary lines and a machine no unnecessary parts. This requires not that the writer make all his sentences short, or that he avoid all detail and treat his subjects only in outline, but that he make every word tell."**

---

### Key Principles

#### 1. Elementary Rules of Usage
- Form possessives correctly (`Charles's friend`, not `Charles' friend`)
- Use a comma before conjunctions in a series (Oxford comma)
- Enclose parenthetic expressions between commas
- Place a comma before a conjunction introducing an independent clause
- Do not join independent clauses with a comma (comma splice); use a semicolon or split into two sentences
- Do not break sentences in two; a period is not a substitute for a comma
- Use the proper case of pronouns
- Use a **colon** after an independent clause to introduce a list, explanation, or amplification
- Use a **semicolon** to join independent clauses not connected by a conjunction
- Use a **dash** to set off an abrupt break or interruption

#### 2. Elementary Principles of Composition
| Principle | Explanation |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Choose a suitable design and hold to it** | Plan before you write |
| **Make the paragraph the unit of composition** | One topic per paragraph; first sentence states it |
| **Use the active voice** | "The dog bit the man" not "The man was bitten by the dog" |
| **Put statements in positive form** | "He was not punctual" → "He was late" |
| **Use definite, specific, concrete language** | Prefer precise words over vague ones |
| **Omit needless words** | Cut clutter ruthlessly |
| **Avoid a succession of loose sentences** | Vary structure; don't string clauses with "and" |
| **Express coordinate ideas in similar form** | Parallel content demands parallel structure |
| **Keep related words together** | Place modifiers near what they modify |
| **In summaries, keep to one tense** | Don't shift tense mid-summary |
| **Place emphatic words at the end** | The final position carries weight |

#### 3. Matters of Form
- Leave a blank line after a title or heading
- Use figures for dates and addresses; spell out numbers under ten
- Avoid starting sentences with numerals
- Hyphenate compound adjectives before nouns
- A sentence within parentheses is not part of the surrounding sentence
- Run short quotes into text; set long quotes as block quotes
- Italicize book titles; use quotes for articles and chapters
- When breaking words at line end, divide on syllable boundaries
- Use consistent formatting throughout

#### 4. Words & Expressions Commonly Misused
Avoid or correct:
- *Aggravate* — means "to make worse," not "to annoy"; use *irritate*
- *Alright* — nonstandard; use *all right*
- *Data* — plural noun; "the data are conclusive"
- *Due to* — adjectival; use "owing to" or "because of" for adverbial
- *Effect / Affect* — *effect* = noun (result); *affect* = verb (influence)
- *Hopefully* — means "in a hopeful manner," not "I hope"; rewrite to clarify
- *However* at the start of a sentence (use sparingly)
- *Less / Fewer* — *less* = quantity; *fewer* = number
- *Nauseous* — means "causing nausea"; use *nauseated* for feeling sick
- *Presently* — means "soon," not "now"; use *currently*
- *That / Which* — *that* = restrictive; *which* = nonrestrictive
- *Utilize* (just say *use*)
- *Very*, *really*, *quite* (usually unnecessary)
- *Rather*, *little*, *pretty* (weak qualifiers)
- *In terms of*, *with regard to* (wordy)
- *The fact that* (wordy; rephrase)
- *Who is*, *which was* (often deletable)
- *Case*, *character*, *nature* (vague fillers)
- *Unique* with modifiers (something is unique or it isn't)

#### 5. An Approach to Style
- **Place yourself in the background** — The reader came for the subject, not the author
- **Write naturally** — Don't imitate; find your own voice
- **Work from a suitable design** — Know where you're going before you start
- **Write with nouns and verbs** — Adjectives and adverbs are not your friends
- **Revise and rewrite** — Writing is rewriting
- **Do not overwrite** — Rich prose weakens with every extra word
- **Do not overstate** — Understatement is more persuasive than exaggeration
- **Avoid qualifiers** — *Rather*, *very*, *little*, *pretty*—cut them
- **Do not affect a breezy manner** — Forced informality rings false
- **Use orthodox spelling** — Novelty spellings distract
- **Do not explain too much** — Trust your reader's intelligence
- **Do not construct awkward adverbs** — *Tiredly*, *overly*—find another way
- **Make sure the reader knows who is speaking** — Dialogue needs clear attribution
- **Avoid fancy words** — Anglo-Saxon over Latinate; *home* over *domicile*
- **Do not use dialect unless your ear is good** — Bad dialect is worse than none
- **Be clear** — Clarity is the paramount virtue
- **Do not inject opinion** — State facts; let readers draw conclusions
- **Use figures of speech sparingly** — Overuse dulls impact
- **Do not take shortcuts at the cost of clarity** — Save the reader, not yourself
- **Avoid foreign languages** — Unless essential, keep it in English
- **Prefer the standard to the offbeat** — Eccentricity for its own sake distracts

- [ ] Does the sentence end strong?
